\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We mapped the configuration space of LLM-based grading for computer science under one controlled
protocol and condensed it into a decision guide. Three results carry the weight. Reasoning is a
conditional trade, not an upgrade: it bought agreement in a minority of model-dataset cells, always
at a large, domain-conditional token premium and almost always at a consistency cost. Its one
reliably explained success was repairing a grader whose baseline had collapsed to zero on code.
Evaluation guidance is the cheapest reliable lever for the model we tested it on, with the caveat that
an unvalidated reference can hide its benefit entirely. And transfer to a real deployment was partial, with only the top of the
public ranking carrying over. Together these justify the framework's governing rule: configuration
priors are starting points, and the deciding measurement is local, against a reference the
institution trusts.

Future work follows the declared gaps. The reasoning by decomposition interaction was not measured;
few-shot example guidance was deliberately excluded and is now justified by the strong guidance
effect, provided it brings its own example-selection protocol; a fine-tuning arm remains open; and
the post-hoc unseen-domain observation awaits confirmatory replication. The framework itself now
feeds the redesign of the assessment platform that motivated it, where its two operational lessons
(traceable grade provenance and a calibrated grading standard) become design requirements rather
than threats.

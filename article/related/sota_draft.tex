\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

Automated grading is a long-standing concern of the learning-analytics and educational-data-mining
communities~\cite{putnikovic_embeddings_2023,ferreira_mello_automatic_2025}. There, it is valued less
as a stand-alone scoring engine than as an assessment \emph{tool} that must be reliable, transparent,
and fair before it can scale formative or summative feedback~\cite{arz_von_straussenburg_enabling_2025,li_am_2023}. A prior
systematic review of the field~\cite{romano_slr_2025} synthesises this trajectory across five eras, from
feature-engineered essay scoring through transformer encoders to the current generation of large language
models (LLMs), and draws one organising conclusion: grading quality is a triad of task, metric, and
deployment, not a single leaderboard. A strong number on one axis (agreement, error, consistency, or
operational cost) does not imply reliability on another. Here we focus on what the most recent
evidence implies for \emph{which configuration} a grader
should use, the question this paper addresses.

\subsection{LLMs as Graders: Capable but Inconsistent}
Prompted LLMs now match or approach human raters on well-specified short-answer and essay
tasks~\cite{aminah_evaluating_2025,sessler_canai_2025,henkel_roars_2025}, yet their reliability is the
recurring caveat. Agreement is strong but depends on the model and the prompt: consistency varies sharply
between systems, with open-source models spanning an intraclass correlation (ICC), the standard
repeated-measurement consistency index, from 0.29 to 0.83 on the same
task~\cite{moazzez_assessing_2024}. Repeated scoring also drifts over time~\cite{pack_large_2024}, and
minimally-prompted grading can stay within one rubric point of teachers yet still yield low summative
agreement~\cite{tate_can_2024}. Two design levers recur as decisive. First, evaluation
\emph{guidance}: clearer, more explicit rubrics raise consistency~\cite{garcia-huertes_leveraging_2024}, and
closed models given a rubric outperform open ones while remaining more lenient than human
raters~\cite{sessler_canai_2025}. Second, \emph{repetition}: because single-shot scores are unstable,
temporal-drift evidence~\cite{pack_large_2024} motivates reporting variance across runs rather than a point
estimate. Reliability is not optional here: grading errors can themselves harm learning, as false positives
and negatives distort how students revise their work~\cite{li_am_2023}. These two observations directly
motivate our evaluation-guidance (RQ2) and consistency (RQ5) axes.

\subsection{Reasoning in Grading: An Open Question}
The headline uncertainty concerns \emph{extended reasoning}. In the general LLM-as-a-judge setting, explicit
reasoning improves judgement. As an example, using a within-family thinking toggle on Qwen3,
``thinking'' models gain roughly ten accuracy points at modest extra cost~\cite{jayarao_explicit_2025}. The benefit, however,
depends on the task and the architecture: on adjacent natural-language-generation evaluation (machine
translation and summarisation), one reasoning model improves with more reasoning while another
\emph{underperforms} its own non-reasoning variant~\cite{larionov_deepseek_2025}. The ``overthinking''
literature adds that long reasoning traces carry verbose, redundant
computation~\cite{sui_stop_2025}. Grading a student answer is calibration-sensitive: it is
bounded by a rubric and sensitive to score calibration rather than to open-ended deliberation. Whether
reasoning helps or hurts here is therefore \emph{a priori} uncertain, and the evidence to settle it is
missing: the studies above measure reasoning in general judging or in adjacent generation tasks, not
in grading. Where reasoning has entered educational grading it has done so only indirectly. Work that
frames short-answer grading as a move ``from memorization to reasoning''~\cite{cong_memorization_2026}
studies fine-tuning and rubric-based prompting rather than isolating the effect of \emph{model}
reasoning, and prior model comparisons confound reasoning with vendor by pitting reasoning and
non-reasoning systems from different families. To our knowledge, no study has isolated the effect of
extended reasoning on grading itself, holding the model fixed and measuring quality, consistency, and
cost together. This paper does so with a within-family on/off toggle (RQ1).

\subsection{Code Grading with Rubrics}
Our code domain is narrower and less mature than short-answer grading. Locally-run open-source LLMs are
already used to \emph{assist} grading in introductory computer science, positioned as teaching-assistant
support rather than as final graders~\cite{mazzone_exploring_2024}. A systematic review of artificial
intelligence in programming education confirms automated assessment and feedback as one of its core
application areas, alongside course design and progress monitoring~\cite{manorat_artificial_2025}.
Rubric-based code graders that award partial credit reach about
85\% micro-accuracy but still mis-score about 15\% of cases, too high for full
automation~\cite{havare_ai-based_2025}. Most relevant to our framing, question-specific rubrics
substantially outperform question-agnostic or no-rubric prompting on object-oriented and data-structures
code (e.g.\ Cohen's $\kappa$ rising from $\approx0.41$ with no rubric to $0.60$--$0.65$ with a
question-specific one)~\cite{pathak_rubric_2025}, tying the evaluation-guidance dimension directly to the
code setting. Because public code datasets with \emph{human} rubric grades are scarce, our code-domain
conclusions are stated more tentatively than the short-answer ones.

\subsection{Datasets and Published Baselines as Context}
We use two established public short-answer corpora, the Mohler dataset of graded computer-science
answers~\cite{mohler_text_2009,mohler_learning_2011} and the SemEval-2013 Task~7 SciEntsBank/Beetle
sets~\cite{dzikovska_semeval_2013}, and confirm transfer on PT-CS, our own Portuguese university
deployment dataset (Section~\ref{sec:datasets}).
Reported numbers are usually quadratic weighted kappa (QWK), an ordinal-agreement score that runs from
0 at chance to 1 at perfect agreement and is defined fully in Section~\ref{sec:metrics}.
Table~\ref{tab:published-baselines} situates our datasets within the published landscape: it gathers
representative results on Mohler, SemEval, and
RIAYN~\cite{aminah_evaluating_2025,chen_cemft_2026,dada_iattention_2025,fischer_evaluation_2025,duong_automatic_2024,ferreira_mello_automatic_2025,kazi_enhancing_2023,henkel_roars_2025},
the nearest supervised benchmark band (ASAP short-answer scoring reaches QWK $\approx0.79$~\cite{kumar_get_2019},
essay scoring $\approx0.83$~\cite{dadi_robust_2025,elmassry_systematic_2025}), and a sample of the wider LLM-as-grader
literature. Crucially, these numbers were produced under \emph{different models, prompts, and
protocols}; they situate our study but are \emph{not} its success criterion, which comes from our own
like-for-like comparisons. The
literature also illustrates why protocol matters: a reported QWK of $0.95$ on Mohler is an artefact of
silently dropping empty or malformed answers~\cite{aminah_evaluating_2025}, underscoring our use of an
extractable-rate ($\pi$) metric and our refusal to coerce unparseable output to zero. Dedicated fine-grained
benchmarks for LLM short-answer scoring are also beginning to appear~\cite{lai_sasbench_2026}, but none yet
isolates the configuration axes we study.

\subsection{From Model Rankings to a Configuration Framework}
A growing body of work compares LLMs \emph{as graders}: large side-by-side studies of programming
submissions~\cite{jukiewicz_comparison_2026}, deployed course evaluations~\cite{policar_automated_2025}, and
multi-model frameworks emphasising transparency and reproducibility~\cite{anghel_graderassist_2025}.
Where these studies compare which model grades best, we ask which configuration a grading task calls
for. Our contribution is a
\emph{configuration framework}: an empirically
grounded decision guide mapping task characteristics (domain, rubric availability, budget) to a recommended
grader configuration and its cost.
